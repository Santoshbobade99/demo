Stock Market Terminology

1. Stock/Share

A unit of ownership in a company.

2. Dividend

A portion of a company's earnings distributed to its shareholders.

3. Market Capitalization (Market Cap)

The total value of a company's outstanding shares, calculated by multiplying the stock price by the number of shares.

4. Bull Market

A market characterized by rising stock prices.

5. Bear Market

A market characterized by falling stock prices.

6. Index

A benchmark that measures the performance of a group of stocks, representing a market or sector.

7. Blue Chip Stocks

Shares of large, stable, and well-established companies with a history of reliable performance.

8. IPO (Initial Public Offering)

The first sale of a company's stock to the public.

9. ETF (Exchange-Traded Fund)

A type of investment fund and exchange-traded product, representing a basket of assets such as stocks, bonds, or commodities.

10. Volatility

The degree of variation of a trading price series over time, indicating the level of risk.

11. Portfolio

A collection of financial assets held by an individual or institution.

12. Diversification

Spreading investments across different assets to reduce risk.

13. Broker

A person or firm that facilitates the buying and selling of financial securities.

14. Market Order

An order to buy or sell a security immediately at the best available current price.

15. Limit Order

An order to buy or sell a security at a specific price or better.

16. Bid and Ask

The bid is the highest price a buyer is willing to pay, and the ask is the lowest price a seller is willing to accept.

17. Stock Split

A corporate action that increases the number of a company's outstanding shares while maintaining its market value.

18. Earnings Per Share (EPS)

A company's profit divided by its outstanding shares.

19. P/E Ratio (Price-to-Earnings Ratio)

A valuation ratio calculated by dividing the market price per share by the earnings per share.

20. Day Trading

The practice of buying and selling financial instruments within the same trading day.

Additional Information

  • Stock Exchange: A marketplace where buyers and sellers trade stocks.
  • Bullish and Bearish: Bullish investors believe the market will rise, while bearish investors expect it to fall.
  • Stock Option: A financial instrument that gives the holder the right to buy or sell a stock at a predetermined price within a specific time frame.
  • Margin Trading: Borrowing funds to buy more stocks than your account's cash balance allows, amplifying both potential gains and losses.
  • Liquidity: The ease with which an asset, like a stock, can be bought or sold in the market without affecting its price.
  • Blue Sky Laws: State regulations designed to protect investors from securities fraud by requiring companies to register their securities offerings.
  • Market Maker: A firm or individual that facilitates trading by providing buy and sell quotes for a financial instrument.
  • Circuit Breaker: Mechanisms in stock exchanges that temporarily halt trading when there are significant price declines to prevent panic selling.
  • Stock Analyst Ratings: Recommendations provided by financial analysts on whether to buy, hold, or sell a particular stock based on their research and analysis.
  • Dividend Yield: The annual dividend income as a percentage of the stock's current market price.
  • Technical Analysis: An approach to evaluating stocks and markets by analyzing statistical trends and trading activity, often using charts and technical indicators.
  • Fundamental Analysis: An evaluation of a stock's intrinsic value by examining financial statements, earnings, and other economic indicators.
  • Short Selling: The practice of selling borrowed stocks with the expectation that their price will decrease, allowing the seller to buy them back at a lower price.
  • Market Capitalization Categories:
    • Micro-Cap: Below $300 million
    • Small-Cap: $300 million to $2 billion
    • Mid-Cap: $2 billion to $10 billion
    • Large-Cap: Above $10 billion
  • Cyclical and Defensive Stocks:
    • Cyclical: Tied to economic cycles, their performance is influenced by economic conditions.
    • Defensive: Tend to remain stable during economic downturns, as they are less sensitive to economic changes.

Remember to conduct thorough research and consider your risk tolerance before making investment decisions.
